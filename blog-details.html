Seed Saving

The Lost Art of Saving Tomato Seeds

October 12, 2023 Dr. Amelia Vance 8 Min Read
Fermentation is crucial for removing the germination-inhibiting gel around tomato seeds.

Saving tomato seeds isn't as simple as squeezing them onto a paper towel and letting them dry. To ensure a high germination rate and kill seed-borne diseases, you must mimic the natural rotting process that occurs in the wild.

The Fermentation Method

Tomato seeds are encased in a gelatinous sac that contains germination inhibitors. If you don't remove this sac, your seeds may rot in the ground rather than sprout next spring. The fermentation process breaks down this gel, effectively mimicking the natural decomposition of a fallen tomato.

"The key to robust heirloom tomatoes lies not in the planting, but in the saving. Fermentation is nature's crucible."

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Select the Best Fruit: Only save seeds from the healthiest, most vigorous plants. Avoid any tomatoes showing signs of disease.
  2. Squeeze and Soak: Cut the tomato across its equator and squeeze the seeds and gel into a small jar. Add a little water if the mixture is too thick.
  3. Wait for the Mold: Leave the jar in a warm spot out of direct sunlight for 3-5 days. A layer of white mold will form on the surface. This is good!
  4. Rinse and Sort: Fill the jar with water and stir vigorously. Good seeds will sink; bad seeds, pulp, and mold will float. Pour off the floaters carefully.
  5. Dry Completely: Spread the clean, sunken seeds on a coffee filter or screen (not paper towel, they will stick!) and let them dry for 1-2 weeks in a cool, dry place.
